01Labor Market Context

NC 10-Year Employment Outlook, 2022–2032

Healthcare & Social Assistance will add more net jobs than any other NC sector — +101,500 over the decade. Professional & Technical Services follows at +62,000. Manufacturing's net growth of +16,000 hides a significant sub-sector divergence: advanced manufacturing growing while legacy textiles and apparel shed thousands of jobs.

02Western NC Labor Market

WNC Employment: Composition, Resilience & Recovery

Western NC's 667,000-job pre-Helene economy was anchored by Education & Health (24.4%) and Manufacturing (16.6%). The hurricane caused an immediate 2.8% employment shock in October 2024, with Leisure & Hospitality losing 15.6% of jobs while Education & Health gained 1.1% — confirming healthcare's counter-cyclical labor market role. Recovery was underway by December but remained incomplete at year-end.

03Healthcare Workforce

Healthcare Occupational Wage Landscape — NC (NAICS 62, 2024)

NC's healthcare sector employs 658,780 workers and is projected to add 101,500 more by 2032 — more than any other sector. The wage distribution reveals a sharp accessibility-wage trade-off: the largest accessible roles (home health aides: 58,730 workers; nursing assistants: 53,460) pay well below living wage, while accessible credential-pathway roles like LPNs, Dental Hygienists, and Radiologic Techs offer strong wage mobility.

NC's healthcare sub-industries vary considerably in wage levels and employment mix. Hospitals (230,600 workers, median $68,320) and Physician Offices (86,480 workers, median $51,720) are the highest-wage sub-sectors. Home Health Care (40,180 workers, median $35,670) and Social Assistance (96,800 workers, median $34,060) anchor the lower end.

Source: BLS OEWS May 2024, NAICS 62 sub-industries, North Carolina.

The education × living wage matrix reveals the most strategic insight for HC workforce investment: at the HS-accessible tier, 80 occupation types (207,800 workers) pay below living wage — versus only 26 (19,230 workers) above. Certificate and associate degree pathways are the most efficient mobility investment: 22 occupation types (69,210 workers) pay above living wage.

Source: BLS OEWS 2024 (NC employment and wages), BLS EP Table 1.2 (education requirements). Living wage = $36,400/yr.
04Advanced Manufacturing Workforce

Advanced Manufacturing Wage Landscape — NC (2023)

NC's advanced manufacturing sector spans eight high-value sub-sectors employing 209,430 workers. Pharmaceutical & Medicine (median $77,730) and Computer & Electronics (median $80,100) lead on wages; Chemical (47,050 workers) and Transportation Equipment (35,490) provide the most jobs. Critically, HS-accessible roles in advanced manufacturing are far more likely to pay above living wage than equivalent-credential healthcare roles.

Source: BLS OEWS 2023, NAICS 325000, 334000, 336000, 325400, 336400, 334500, 334400, 333000, 335000, North Carolina.

In advanced manufacturing, 50 HS-accessible occupation types (63,210 workers) pay above living wage — compared to just 29 below. Every certificate/associate credential manufacturing occupation pays above living wage. This inverts the healthcare pattern and makes manufacturing the stronger wage-mobility bet for workers without four-year degrees.

06Geographic Labor Markets

Unemployment Rates, All 100 NC Counties — 2024 Annual Average

Two structurally different unemployment stories coexist in NC. Eastern NC counties (Hyde 5.6%, Edgecombe/Halifax/Scotland 5.2%) show persistently elevated unemployment rooted in long-running economic challenges. Separately, Helene's statistical footprint is quantifiable: the 26 Permanent Work counties averaged +0.34pp higher unemployment YoY vs. +0.01pp for the rest of the state — Buncombe (+1.4pp), Mitchell (+1.1pp), and Avery (+1.0pp) absorbed the sharpest shocks.

Source: NC Commerce LEAD, BLS LAUS 2024 annual averages (not seasonally adjusted). NC statewide 2024 annual average: 3.7%. Red = Helene Permanent Work county.
07Apprenticeship System

ApprenticeshipNC: Growth, Gaps & the Funding Cliff

NC's registered apprenticeship system is growing fast but remains a tiny fraction of actual workforce demand — especially in healthcare (0.5% of estimated annual openings) and manufacturing (1.3%). The program's momentum is fueled by federal grants that expire in June 2026. Without $3.1M in annual state investment, active participation could fall from 10,255 to ~4,000 by 2034.

Data Sources & Methodology

Labor Market Data

BLS Quarterly Census of Employment & Wages (QCEW) via NC Commerce LEAD; BLS Local Area Unemployment Statistics (LAUS) 2024 annual averages via FRED; BLS Occupational Employment & Wage Statistics (OEWS) May 2024 (NAICS 62) and May 2023 (NAICS 31–33). NC LEAD 2022–2032 Long-Term Employment Projections.

Education & Occupation Data

BLS Employment Projections Table 1.2 (2024–34): occupational projections, education requirements, on-the-job training, annual openings, and median wages for 832 detailed occupations. Living wage benchmark: MIT Living Wage Calculator 2024, single adult, North Carolina (~$17.50/hr, $36,400/yr).

Apprenticeship Data

ApprenticeshipNC Annual Reports FY 2018–2025 (submitted per NCGS 115D-11.6 to the NC General Assembly). Helene impact data: NC Commerce LEAD Hurricane Helene labor market analysis, July 2025. Population: NC OSBM county estimates, July 2024. Demand estimates are directional proxies using national openings × NC employment share.
